This is a story of how fair rules can create an unfair world.

Meet Alice and Bob. They each start with $100. They're about to play a simple game — just flipping a coin.

Here are the rules. Each round, they flip a coin. The stake is always a percentage of the poorer person's wealth:

Those rates aren't arbitrary — 20% up and 16.67% down are exactly balanced. If the coin flips perfectly alternated H, T, H, T, wealth would never change at all:

Every time, there's almost always a large disparity!

To be clear: the sim does let the poor and rich swap places. Watch the two-person auto sim long enough and you'll see the leader change several times — sometimes Alice is on top, sometimes Bob. The point isn't that one specific person stays rich forever. It's that there is almost always a large gap between whoever is currently on top and whoever is on the bottom. Someone always ends up very rich, and someone always ends up very poor, even when the rules are identical for both.

And this isn't just two people. Watch what happens with a whole society.

50 people, all starting equal. hit play:

Starting from perfect equality, with fair coin flips, you get a handful of people with almost all the wealth and everyone else with almost nothing.

The inequality score (where 0% = everyone equal, 100% = one person has everything) climbs relentlessly toward 100%.

Fair rules + random luck = unfair outcomes.

But here's the hopeful part: we can fix it.

One idea: what if we change the rules so the poorer person loses even less on a bad flip? Like, what if instead of losing 16.67%, they only lost 15%? Or 10%?

try lowering the loss fraction:

Interesting! The default 16.67% is essentially balanced — (technically 16.6 repeating, i.e. 1/6, is the exact break-even point) — and at that level, inequality still explodes. But drop the loss to around 15%, and suddenly inequality does stay flat. A mild tilt toward the poor is enough to overcome multiplicative dynamics.

So this is a real solution! The catch: in the real world, telling people "you have to lose 5% less than you fairly should whenever you're the poorer party in a trade" is... a tough sell. It requires rewriting the rules of every transaction in the economy, which isn't exactly practical.

There's another approach that's a lot more direct: redistribution.

What if we leave the trade rules alone, and just have everyone chip in a tiny wealth tax that gets split equally among everyone? In this sim, the tax is collected every N flips (where N is the number of people — so every 50 flips with 50 people). That way, on average, each person participates in two trades between tax rounds (since every flip involves two people).

try adding a wealth tax. even a tiny one:

Whoa. Even a tiny wealth tax completely changes the outcome. Instead of one person ending up with everything, the wealth distribution stays relatively flat. The inequality score stabilizes instead of climbing to 100%.

The key insight: redistribution counteracts multiplicative dynamics directly. A small flow of wealth from the top back to the bottom is enough to prevent runaway concentration — without having to radically rewrite the trade rules themselves.

But wait — what about the real world? We don't start from a clean slate. Inequality already exists. Can a wealth tax fix it even after the damage is done?

the world starts with extreme inequality. try adding a wealth tax:

Yes! Even starting from extreme inequality, the wealth tax gradually pulls things back toward a more equal distribution. It doesn't happen instantly — but given enough time, redistribution overcomes the initial imbalance.

The past doesn't have to haunt the present. The same small mechanism that prevents inequality from forming can also undo inequality that already exists.

WRAPPING UP:

1. Fair rules can create unfair worlds.
You don't need cheating, corruption, or conspiracy to get extreme inequality.
(not to say that isn't happening, but that's a topic for another time)

2. Inequality isn't (just) about bad actors.
The structure of the system matters as much as individual behavior. Even a perfectly balanced game still concentrates wealth at the top.

3. Small redistribution makes a big difference.
Even a tiny wealth tax is enough to prevent runaway inequality!

WHAT CAN YOU DO?

No one person can fix systemic inequality alone. But understanding how it works is the first step. Here's what you can do:

1. Spread the word.
Share this page and inform other people of this issue. Again, we can't do anything alone!

2. Advocate for progressive taxation.
The simulations show that even a tiny wealth tax dramatically flattens runaway concentration. So, vote for policymakers that support wealth taxes or more progressive income taxes!

3. Remember: it's fixable.
The same principles that show inequality is inevitable also show it's reversible. We don't need to change everything. Just a little redistribution is enough. The hard part are the politics.
